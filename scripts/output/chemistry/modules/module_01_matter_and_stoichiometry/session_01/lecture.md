## Main Topic 1: States of Matter

Matter exists in several distinct states, each characterized by its unique physical properties. The three most commonly encountered states are solid, liquid, and gas. Let’s begin with a simple analogy: consider water. It can exist as ice (solid), water (liquid), or steam (gas), all of which share the same chemical formula – H₂O – but display drastically different behaviors.

*   **Solid:** In a solid state, the particles (atoms or molecules) are tightly packed together in a fixed arrangement, giving the substance a definite shape and volume. Think of a diamond – its rigid structure allows it to maintain its shape regardless of its environment.
*   **Liquid:** Liquids have a fixed volume but take the shape of their container. The particles are still close together but have more freedom to move past one another. Imagine pouring water into a glass; it fills the glass, but it maintains a constant volume.
*   **Gas:** Gases have neither a definite shape nor a definite volume; they expand to fill whatever container they are in. The particles in a gas move randomly and independently of each other. Consider the example of helium escaping from a balloon – it expands until it reaches atmospheric pressure.

These states aren’t necessarily exclusive; matter can transition between states through processes like melting (solid to liquid), freezing (liquid to solid), boiling (liquid to gas), or condensation (gas to liquid).

## Main Topic 2: Physical vs. Chemical Changes

Now, let's distinguish between two fundamental types of changes we observe in matter: physical and chemical.

A **physical change** alters the form or appearance of a substance, but *does not* change its chemical composition. This means the substance is still the same substance after the change. For instance, cutting a piece of wood – it's still wood. Another example is dissolving sugar in water; the sugar disappears, but it’s still sugar molecules, just dispersed among water molecules.  Adding salt to water is a physical change – the salt dissolves, but it remains salt.  A further example is crushing an aluminum can – it's still aluminum, just in a different shape.

Conversely, a **chemical change** (also known as a chemical reaction) involves the formation of *new* substances with different properties. This happens when atoms rearrange themselves to form new molecules. A classic example is burning wood. The wood reacts with oxygen to produce ash, carbon dioxide, and water – substances entirely different from the original wood. Another example is rusting iron – the iron reacts with oxygen and water to form iron oxide (rust), a completely different material. Cooking an egg is a chemical change – the proteins denature and form new compounds.

## Main Topic 3: Properties of Matter

Matter can be described using various properties, which can be broadly classified into physical and chemical properties.

**Physical Properties** are those that can be observed or measured without changing the chemical composition of the substance. These include:

*   **Density**:  A measure of mass per unit volume. For instance, lead is denser than aluminum, meaning a given volume of lead will have more mass than the same volume of aluminum.
*   **Melting Point**: The temperature at which a solid transitions to a liquid.
*   **Boiling Point**: The temperature at which a liquid transitions to a gas.
*   **Color**: The appearance of the substance when light is reflected.
*   **Texture**: How the substance feels to the touch (e.g., smooth, rough, hard, soft).

**Chemical Properties** describe how a substance reacts with other substances.  These properties are revealed only when a chemical reaction occurs.  For instance, flammability describes a substance’s ability to burn, while reactivity describes how readily it reacts with other substances – such as acids or bases.

## Key Concepts: Mixture, Pure Substance, Compound

Let’s now explicitly address the key concepts introduced: mixture, pure substance, and compound.

A **Mixture** is a combination of two or more substances that are physically combined but not chemically bonded.  The individual substances retain their own properties. Examples include air (a mixture of nitrogen, oxygen, and other gases), saltwater (water and salt), and sand mixed with gravel. Mixtures can be separated by physical means, such as filtration or distillation.

A **Pure Substance** is a sample of matter that contains only one type of particle – either an element or a compound. It has a definite composition and distinct physical properties.

A **Compound** is a pure substance consisting of two or more *different* elements chemically combined in a fixed ratio. The elements lose their original properties and form a new substance with different properties.  Water (H₂O) is a compound – hydrogen and oxygen combine to form a substance with properties distinct from those of hydrogen or oxygen alone.  Sodium chloride (NaCl), common table salt, is another example – it’s a compound formed from sodium and chlorine.  Carbon dioxide (CO₂) is also a compound.
