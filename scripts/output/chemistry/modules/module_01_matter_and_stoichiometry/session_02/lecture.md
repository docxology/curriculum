## Main Topic 1: The Components of the Atom

The atom, as initially conceived, was considered to be indivisible – the smallest unit of matter. However, through advances in physics, particularly the work of J.J. Thomson and Ernest Rutherford, we’ve discovered that atoms are, in fact, composed of even smaller components. Let’s examine these components in detail.

### 1.1 Protons and Neutrons – The Nucleus

At the heart of every atom lies a dense, positively charged region called the **nucleus** ( **nucleus**: The central core of an atom containing protons and neutrons). This nucleus accounts for almost all of the atom’s mass. The nucleus is comprised of two types of subatomic particles: protons and neutrons.

*   **Protons**: These particles carry a positive electrical charge (+1) and contribute to the atom’s atomic number. The number of protons defines the element. For instance, all atoms with one proton are hydrogen atoms.
*   **Neutrons**: These particles have no electrical charge (neutral) and contribute significantly to the atom’s mass. The number of neutrons can vary within an element, leading to different isotopes (we’ll discuss this further shortly).

Consider the example of carbon. Every carbon atom has 6 protons. That's what makes it carbon!

---

## Main Topic 2: Electrons – Orbiting the Nucleus

Surrounding the nucleus are negatively charged particles called **electrons** ( **electron**: A subatomic particle with a negative electrical charge, orbiting the nucleus). Electrons are much lighter than protons and neutrons and occupy regions around the nucleus known as **electron shells** ( **electron shell**: An orbital or region around the nucleus where electrons are likely to be found; energy levels). These shells are often visualized as layers around the nucleus, with each shell holding a specific number of electrons.

The arrangement of electrons within the electron shells determines how an atom will interact with other atoms – essentially, how it will form chemical bonds. Electrons aren't just randomly orbiting; they exist in specific energy levels, and transitions between these levels involve the absorption or emission of energy, typically in the form of light. This is the basis of atomic spectra.

Imagine a solar system, with the nucleus as the sun and electrons orbiting it like planets. However, unlike planets, electrons don't follow neat, predictable paths. Their locations are described by probability distributions.

## Main Topic 3: Atomic Number and Mass Number

Now let's define two key values associated with each atom: the atomic number and the mass number.

*   **Atomic Number**: This is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom. It uniquely identifies an element. For example, all atoms with an atomic number of 8 are oxygen atoms.
*   **Mass Number**: This is the total number of protons and neutrons in the nucleus of an atom. It’s a numerical representation of the atom's mass. For instance, an atom with 6 protons and 6 neutrons would have a mass number of 12.

It's important to note that the mass number doesn't always directly represent the atomic mass listed on the periodic table. This is because the periodic table uses *average* atomic masses, taking into account the natural abundance of isotopes.

Consider the element iron (Fe). Iron has an atomic number of 26, meaning it always has 26 protons. It typically has 56 neutrons, giving it a mass number of 82.

## Main Topic 4: Isotopes

Because the number of neutrons can vary, atoms of the same element can exist in different forms called **isotopes** ( **isotope**: Atoms of the same element that have different numbers of neutrons, leading to different mass numbers).  All carbon atoms have six protons – this is the defining characteristic of carbon. However, some carbon atoms have six neutrons, while others have seven or eight. These are carbon-12, carbon-13, and carbon-14, respectively.  All of these are still carbon because they all possess the same atomic number.

The varying neutron numbers lead to differences in atomic mass.  Stable isotopes exist, while unstable isotopes undergo radioactive decay, transforming into different elements.

Imagine a set of coins - all are ‘dollars’ (same atomic number), but have different weights (different mass numbers – isotopes).

## Main Topic 5: Calculating Atomic Number and Mass

Let’s practice calculating the atomic number and mass number for a few elements.

*   **Example 1:** An atom has 17 protons and 18 neutrons.
    *   Atomic Number = 17 (as it has 17 protons)
    *   Mass Number = 17 + 18 = 35
    *   This is chlorine (Cl).
*   **Example 2:** An atom has 8 protons and 9 neutrons.
    *   Atomic Number = 8
    *   Mass Number = 8 + 9 = 17
    *   This is oxygen (O).
