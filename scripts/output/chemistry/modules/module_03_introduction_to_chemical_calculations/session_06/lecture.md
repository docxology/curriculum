## Main Topic 1: Step-by-Step Problem Solving

Solving stoichiometry problems requires a structured approach. There isn’t a ‘one-size-fits-all’ method, but a consistent process will dramatically improve your accuracy and efficiency. Here’s a recommended sequence:

1.  **Read the Problem Carefully:** Identify what is given and what is asked for. Pay close attention to units. Misunderstanding the question is a common source of error.
2.  **Write a Balanced Equation:** This is absolutely critical. Ensure the equation is correctly balanced. Remember, the coefficients in a balanced equation represent the *mole* ratios between the reactants and products.
3.  **Convert Units (If Necessary):** Often, the given quantities are in units other than moles (e.g., grams, liters). You’ll need to convert to moles using molar mass. For instance, to convert grams of a substance to moles, use the formula: moles = mass / molar mass.
4.  **Set Up a Mole Ratio:** This is the heart of stoichiometry. Use the balanced equation to establish the ratio between the known and unknown quantities.
5.  **Solve for the Unknown:** Perform the necessary calculations using the established mole ratio.
6.  **Check Your Answer:**  Does your answer make sense in the context of the problem? Does the unit of your answer align with what’s being asked?

Let’s consider an example:  “If 10.0 g of sodium chloride (NaCl) is dissolved in water, what mass of chlorine gas (Cl₂) is produced?”

## Main Topic 2: Applying the Mole Concept

The **mole**: The mole is a unit of measurement that represents a specific number of particles – atoms, molecules, or ions. One mole contains Avogadro's number (6.022 x 10²³) of these particles. The mole is a bridge between the macroscopic world (grams) and the microscopic world (atoms/molecules).  Using the mole allows us to relate the amounts of substances in a chemical reaction.

Imagine you have a container filled with 100 marbles. You could say you have "one hundred marbles." However, it's more precise to say you have "one mole of marbles," where each mole contains 6.022 x 10²² marbles.  Similarly, in chemical reactions, the mole is the fundamental unit for quantifying reactants and products.

To calculate the number of moles from a given mass, we use the **molar mass** which is calculated by adding up the atomic masses of each element in the compound. For example, the molar mass of water (H₂O) is (2 * 1.01 g/mol) + (1 * 16.00 g/mol) = 18.02 g/mol.

Consider the reaction:  2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s).  This equation tells us that 2 moles of magnesium react with 1 mole of oxygen to produce 2 moles of magnesium oxide.  The coefficients directly represent the mole ratios.

For instance, if we have 48.0 g of magnesium, we can calculate the number of moles: moles = 48.0 g / 24.31 g/mol = 1.98 moles (approximately).

## Main Topic 3: Example Problem 1

Let's work through a detailed example to solidify these concepts.

**Problem:**  “What mass of carbon dioxide (CO₂) is produced when 4.46 g of methane (CH₄) burns completely in oxygen?”

**Solution:**

1.  **Balanced Equation:** CH₄(g) + 2O₂(g) → CO₂(g) + 2H₂O(g)
2.  **Calculate Moles of Methane:** moles of CH₄ = 4.46 g / 16.05 g/mol = 0.278 mol (approximately)
3.  **Mole Ratio:** From the balanced equation, 1 mole of CH₄ produces 1 mole of CO₂.
4.  **Calculate Moles of CO₂:** moles of CO₂ = 0.278 mol
5.  **Calculate Mass of CO₂:** moles of CO₂ x molar mass of CO₂ = 0.278 mol x 44.01 g/mol = 12.2 g (approximately).  Therefore, 12.2 g of carbon dioxide is produced.
