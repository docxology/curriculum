## Main Topic 1: Reactants and Products

A **chemical equation** is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction. It’s written in a specific format to accurately depict the substances involved. The key components are **reactants** and **products**.

*   **Reactants**: These are the starting materials in a chemical reaction. They are the substances that are consumed during the reaction. They are written on the left side of the chemical equation.
*   **Products**: These are the substances formed as a result of the reaction. They are written on the right side of the chemical equation.

For instance, consider the reaction of hydrogen gas (H₂) with oxygen gas (O₂) to form water (H₂O). We can represent this reaction as:

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Here, H₂ and O₂ are the reactants, and H₂O is the product. The arrow (→) indicates the direction of the reaction – the transformation from reactants to products.

## Main Topic 2: Balancing Equations

The fundamental principle governing chemical reactions is the **Law of Conservation of Mass**, which states that matter cannot be created or destroyed in a chemical reaction. This means the number of atoms of each element must remain the same before and after the reaction. Therefore, chemical equations must be *balanced* to ensure this conservation. Balancing an equation involves adjusting the coefficients (the numbers placed in front of chemical formulas) to make the number of atoms of each element equal on both sides of the equation.

Consider the equation:

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

This equation is unbalanced because there are 2 nitrogen atoms on the left and only 1 on the right. To balance it, we must place a coefficient in front of NH₃.  We need two NH₃ molecules to have two nitrogen atoms. The balanced equation becomes:

N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃

Now, we have 2 nitrogen atoms on both sides. It’s crucial to remember that we *never* change the subscripts within a chemical formula because that would change the identity of the substance. Instead, we adjust the coefficients.

## Main Topic 3: Types of Reactions – Synthesis

A **synthesis reaction** is a reaction where two or more reactants combine to form a single product.  A classic example is the formation of water from hydrogen and oxygen:

H₂ + O₂ → H₂O

Notice the single product and the combination of reactants.

---

## Main Topic 4: Types of Reactions – Decomposition

A **decomposition reaction** is the reverse of synthesis – a single reactant breaks down into two or more products. For instance, the decomposition of calcium carbonate (CaCO₃) into calcium oxide (CaO) and carbon dioxide (CO₂) is represented as:

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂

Here, one reactant breaks down into two products. This type of reaction often requires heat to proceed.

---

## Main Topic 5: Types of Reactions – Single Displacement

A **single displacement reaction**, also called a single replacement reaction, occurs when one element replaces another in a compound. Consider the reaction between zinc metal (Zn) and hydrochloric acid (HCl):

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂

Zinc (a more reactive metal) displaces hydrogen from HCl, forming zinc chloride (ZnCl₂) and hydrogen gas (H₂).

## Main Topic 6: Key Concepts – Chemical Formula and Balanced Equation

Let’s reinforce the key concepts. The **chemical formula** (e.g., H₂O, NaCl) represents a specific compound and defines its composition. The **balanced equation** precisely describes a chemical reaction, ensuring the conservation of mass and accurately reflecting the stoichiometry – the quantitative relationship between reactants and products.  As we’ve seen, balancing requires adjusting coefficients, but the chemical formulas themselves remain constant. For example, a balanced equation for the combustion of methane (CH₄) is:

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

This shows the complete conversion of methane into carbon dioxide and water.
